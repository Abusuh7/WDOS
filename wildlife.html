Yala-:

Yala National Park was established as a protected area on Sri Lanka's south east coast in 1938. It's a massive reserve with a broad range of ecosystems, including open parkland, dense jungle, flooded lagoons, and high coastal sand dunes. A wide range of plants and animals can be found in this diversified area.

Sinharaja-:

Sinharaja Forest Reserve is Sri Lanka's last remaining primary tropical rainforest, featuring flowing waterfalls, bubbling pools, and green ferns. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and Biosphere Reserve. In the south west of the island, the canopy of the forest rises to 45 meters, and over two-thirds of the trees are endemic, with many of them considered rare. Nonetheless, one of the main reasons to visit Sinharaja is for the excellent birdwatching. More than 80% of the island's bird species call the reserve home, including endemics like the green-billed coucal, blue magpie, and red-faced malkoha.

Udawalewe-:

Udawalawe National Park, located just south of Sri Lanka's central mountains, is a massive 119-square-mile reserve of grassland, scrub jungle, and riverine forest. The park's most popular attraction is its vast population of Asian elephants; Udawalawe is home to roughly 400 of these gentle giants, and seeing them on safari is almost guaranteed. During periods of water scarcity, elephant herds assemble at the Walawe Reservoir to drink. The mugger crocodile, sambar, spotted and barking deer, wild boar, water buffalo, and jackal, as well as around 200 kinds of birds, are some of the other noteworthy fauna present in Udawalawe National Park. Birdwatching trips are perfect for observing unusual raptors such as the changeable hawk-eagle, serpent eagle, and grey-headed fish eagle from November through March.

Mirissa-:

Mirissa, a small beach town on Sri Lanka's south coast, is the most popular whale-watching site in the country. Whales breaching in the pounding surf are a rare sight, and Mirissa is the best place in Sri Lanka to watch them. Meet these amazing species up up and personal by riding into the deep water with a reputable and environmentally conscious company. There's a considerable chance you'll encounter blue whales, as well as sperm, fin, and killer whales. From May to October is the ideal time to watch whales. During a whale watching cruise, you may witness a variety of dolphins as well as mesmerizing sea turtles bobbing along the shore.Hikkaduwa Marine National Park

Hikkaduwa Maine-:

Hikkaduwa National Park, in Sri Lanka's southern district, was first established as a Nature Reserve in 1988, and then as a National Park in 2002. It's one of the country's only two maritime national parks. Although there are several coral reefs around the southern coast, the reefs at Hikkaduwa are the most diverse and accessible, offering some of the country's best snorkeling and diving opportunities. The main reef at Hikkaduwa is a typical fringing reef with an average depth of around 5 meters and is located in a sheltered lagoon. The coral is lively and diverse, with around 60 different species including boulder and encrusting corals, thin strands of Acropora coral, and the cabbage-like Montipora coral. Tropical reef fish such as parrot fish, angel fish, and butterfly fish dart between the corals, while sea turtles lay their eggs in the seagrass. Along the reef's outer slope, blacktip reef sharks, as well as hawksbill and green turtles, can be seen.

Minneriya National Park-:

Minneriya National Park is a small park in Sri Lanka's north central area, encompassing just over 300 square kilometers. The reserve features a varied range of habitats, including grasslands and heavy scrub, dry tropical forest, and wetlands, despite its small area. Minneriya also features a man-made tank that filters water for the reservoir and feeds the park's animals during the dry months. Deer, purple-faced langur and macaque monkeys, sloth bears, and over 20 leopards, as well as a broad range of migratory and indigenous birds such as the hanging parrot, brown-capped babbler, and green bee-eater, have been sighted in Minneriya. 'The Gathering,' a sight in which over 300 elephants converge at the Minneriya reservoir in search of water and green grass, is the reserve's main attraction during the dry season (July till October). This magnificent assembly of Asian elephants is the world's largest.
